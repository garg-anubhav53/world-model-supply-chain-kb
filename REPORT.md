## Where we stand (honest)
Another wide, low-yield sourcing fire — eight scouts plus three Opus dives, roughly twenty found-and-killed candidates, one new private lead with no listing path — but the conviction track did real work: a third genuine re-derivation of Airgain found the score unchanged while the underlying picture quietly got worse, which is exactly the kind of thing a status-check-only cadence would have missed.

Airgain (AIRG) got its third genuine full 4-gate re-derivation since the Coco Robotics design win first surfaced in March. The number hasn't moved — 3 out of 8, same as Fires 36 and 42 — but the composition of that number has. A cluster of four sell-side analysts (Craig-Hallum, Roth Capital, Northland, Lake Street) raised their price targets on June 24, converging around an $8.88 fair value, and every one of them is pricing the FirstNet/MegaFi connectivity turnaround, not the robotics story. That matters because it means the stock's "not yet discovered" gate — the thing that made this interesting in the first place — is closing regardless of whether Coco Robotics ever produces a dollar figure. And after four months, it still hasn't: the Q1 10-Q doesn't mention Coco or robotics anywhere, gross margin ticked down on an unfavorable mix, and the only new context (a departed CTO quietly winding down his position via a handful of small Form 144 filings) is unrelated noise, not a red flag. The upshot: Airgain moves to the bottom of the shortlist, and the Aug 7-11 earnings call is now an explicit, dated line in the sand — no Coco/VTOL number by then, and next fire should treat this as a live kill candidate.

The two on-deck names both got pushed again and both stayed exactly where they were, for good reasons this time rather than stalled ones. MicroVision's shareholder vote on its reverse split and convertible-note issuance is scheduled for July 10 — one calendar day after this fire's check — so "no outcome yet" is a fact about the calendar, not a research gap; the item to watch is the post-meeting Item 5.07 filing, which should land within a few business days. Its buyer (unofficially Caterpillar, per trade press) is still unnamed in any company filing, and a capital-markets filing that looked like fresh money turned out to just be a re-registration of an existing ATM facility — nothing changed there either. AEye's case actually got marginally weaker: on its Q1 call, management walked back the near-term significance of its ~$30 million design win, saying the revenue is "further out in time" than previously implied. Neither promotes. AEye now has its first dedicated dossier in the KB, since it had only ever been tracked as scattered inline notes before.

Sourcing ran across seven vectors and mostly reconfirmed how thin the well has gotten. The rotation cursor landed on defense/drone autonomy for a fresh-trigger check — the vertical's been tested three times before and stayed tested; all 19 finalists in the Pentagon's Gauntlet II drone competition are mapped, only one has a public listing vehicle (already tracked), and Neros Technologies, the most obvious pre-IPO candidate, still hasn't filed anything despite raising over $100 million. With nothing fresh there, the idle-dimension override retested two older near-misses: ECARX's revenue finally crossed the $800 million size cap (closing that thread for good), and a second look at two Chinese magnet makers found one of them now explicitly denies working with Unitree at all.

The more interesting failures this fire were pattern-level, not name-level. Two separate Korean and Japanese small-caps — Hwashin Precision and Hephaist Seiko — had genuinely real, dated, named-buyer-adjacent disclosures (a real Boston Dynamics factory tour for the former, a real humanoid-consortium membership for the latter) that were already priced in 200-370% before this fire could find them. That's a "sell the news" pattern distinct from the China A-share IPO-front-running pattern already in the KB, and it's now the second time a supplier-evaluation story alone (not even a signed order) has moved a thin-float stock this hard. Separately, a single EDGAR sweep for "Zhiyuan"-related filings turned up four entirely unrelated companies that happen to share that name fragment — a PE fund subsidiary, an HVAC consultancy, a crypto-mining-hardware unit, and an individual patent inventor — confirming that this specific name-collision problem (first spotted with Infobird back in Fire 36) needs a standing identity check, not a one-off fix.

The one genuinely new thread worth tracking: tracing one hop past Nuttall (already a known private PENDING name, itself Zhiyuan's largest customer by revenue) found its own largest bearing supplier, Luoyang Hongyuan Bearing Technology, which by its own disclosure holds more than 90% of China's domestic robot-bearing market. That's a real, dominant, "hidden champion" position — but it's private, and the one rumored path to a public listing (a reverse merger into an already-listed shell) was directly denied by that shell company. Logged for IPO-watch, not actionable today.

(3) "Zhiyuan"-name-collision on EDGAR is now a confirmed recurring pattern (4 new instances in one sweep) — every hit needs an identity check before being logged as a lead. (4) A two-hop trace through an already-known private PENDING name's own supplier disclosures can surface a genuine "hidden champion," but expect it to be private with no listing path more often than not.
