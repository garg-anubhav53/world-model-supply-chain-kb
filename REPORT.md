## Where we stand (honest)
The drought broke this fire — two new names cleared the widened WATCH bar, each found via a genuinely different non-anchored discovery method, and neither is a clean story yet. That's the right way for this to happen: both carry one explicit, named, unresolved gate rather than reading as settled conclusions.

**Intops Co Ltd (KOSDAQ: 049070)** came out of an ImportYeti customs retry on Bear Robotics, the LG Electronics-acquired serving-robot maker whose supplier table had truncated mid-fetch last fire — a genuinely transient tooling issue, not a durable block, since a plain retry this time reached the full page. Eighty-four percent of Bear Robotics' 56 recorded shipments route through Intops, and the product descriptions on those shipments read, verbatim, "Serving Robot," "Indoor Serving Robots," and "AMR" parts. That's about as unambiguous as a customs-record causal link gets — no press inference required, no "aggregator claims" hedge. The company itself is a legacy Samsung-phone-parts EMS supplier that's pivoted into what it calls a "wearable robot manufacturing platform," trading at roughly half of book value and down 44% off its own 52-week high, having just returned to profitability after five straight loss quarters. What's genuinely unresolved is materiality: Intops' total revenue base is large enough (~$480M+ annualized) relative to its ~$200-235M market cap that the robotics slice could easily be a rounding error against a phone-parts business that still dominates — exactly the shape of trap that's killed a dozen-plus China A-share names in this KB. That's the single question to chase next fire.

**Woorim P&ES (KOSDAQ: 101170)** is a more delicate case. It surfaced as a fourth name in the same Boston Dynamics Atlas Korean-supplier evaluation story that produced Hwashin Precision and Hephaist Seiko last fire — both of which got killed specifically because they'd already re-rated 200-370% on the disclosure alone, a pattern the KB now treats as the default prior for any fresh Korea/Japan buyer-evaluation headline. Woorim broke that pattern: it had its own sell-the-news spike in December (a run to ₩24,000 on CES-2026 Atlas anticipation) but has since round-tripped almost all the way back to its 52-week low. So the price gate is genuinely clean here in a way it wasn't for the other two names in the same story — which is itself the interesting finding, that "the whole cluster is priced" doesn't hold uniformly and each name needs its own check. What's unresolved is disclosure quality: the reducer/gearbox-supplier claim traces only to Korean press and aggregator coverage, not yet to a primary source (the company's own filing, or a statement from Boston Dynamics or Hyundai). That's the same distinction that walked back Xinjian Transmission's promotion at Fire 43 — a media-inferred buyer name is a real find, but a weaker one than a filed disclosure, and it needs to be checked before this graduates past "worth watching."

The conviction track did its own real work this fire. HSAI got its fourth genuine full re-derivation since being tracked — six fires since the last one, Fire 40 — and it's the sharpest example yet of a lesson worth stating explicitly: a name's underlying business case and its asymmetry as an investment idea can move in opposite directions on the same re-derivation. On the business side, this was the best evidence update HSAI has had: Hesai is now the named primary/default lidar partner for NVIDIA's DRIVE Hyperion 10 platform, on top of a new Mercedes-Benz supply deal — genuinely stronger and broader than anything found at Fire 40. The 4-gate score moved up a full point, to 7 of 8. But at a true market cap of roughly $20.6 billion (computed by hand again this fire, since the market-cap aggregators are still all showing different wrong numbers post-split) and around 40 times revenue with 21 analysts rated Strong Buy, this is now a thoroughly discovered, consensus name — the "nobody's looked at this yet" quality that made it asymmetric in the first place is what's actually eroding, even as the demand story gets better. It stays the KB's #1 conviction WATCH, with an explicit new flag that the two axes — thesis strength and asymmetry — need to be tracked separately from here on.

Sourcing ran across the usual wide set of vectors and mostly reconfirmed how picked-over most of them are. The rotation cursor landed on royalty/IP-licensing for a fourth consecutive idle check — Fallbrook Technologies and Genesis Robotics are still private, Ouster's own royalty line actually got worse (down to $0.3M this quarter from $1.5M a year ago), and this is now, on the numbers, the single most exhausted corner of the whole framework; it's worth checking on a much longer cadence going forward rather than every fire. China A-share and Chinese trade-press sweeps each found two real but immaterial or unconfirmed leads and nothing that clears every gate — the pattern there continues to be either an explicit company self-denial or a claim that traces back to nothing sturdier than a retail-investor forum post. The EDGAR sweep's main contribution this fire was cataloguing, not discovering: six more instances of the "Zhiyuan"-name-collision problem (an award name, a PE-fund subsidiary, a school, an individual's name, a tax-services unit, a shareholder partnership), plus two brand-new false-positive shapes worth a standing filter — a SPAC's own risk-factor competitor list that will keep generating false hits on five different buyer names every time this sweep runs, and a CFO's biography mentioning a past employer that isn't a business relationship at all.

## Standing lesson this fire
**Two lessons.** (1) A staleness-guard re-derivation can find a name's business thesis genuinely de-risking (more gates passing, stronger evidence) at the same time its asymmetry quality is declining (less cheap/unknown, more consensus/crowded) — these are different axes and don't have to move together. HSAI is now the KB's clearest example: objectively the best evidence update it's ever had, and simultaneously less of an asymmetric idea than it was six fires ago, purely on valuation/crowding grounds. (2) "Presumptively already-priced" is the right starting prior for a freshly-surfaced Korea/Japan buyer-evaluation story, but it has to be verified per-name — the same underlying buyer-tour disclosure left one supplier (Hwashin) permanently re-rated 270%+ while an adjacent one (Woorim) round-tripped back to its 52-week low within months.
