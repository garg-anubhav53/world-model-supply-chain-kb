## Where we stand (honest)
This was a wide-sourcing, low-yield fire in terms of new names — eleven scouts plus two Opus dives, seventeen found-and-killed candidates, one weak new lead — but it did land one genuine binding-gate decision, and it closed out a piece of standing KB business that had been open since Fire 41.

NOVT (Novanta, owner of ATI's force-torque sensor line) got its third genuine full 4-gate re-derivation, the last of four legacy WATCH placeholders (after ON Semiconductor, Klingelnberg, and TKR/Spinea, all killed in Fires 41-43) to get this treatment. Unlike those three, NOVT actually has real, live robotics exposure — a real force-torque product line marketed specifically at humanoids, an unnamed multi-year Amazon warehouse-automation win, and Robotics & Automation segment bookings up 50% year-over-year — so this isn't a kill. But the picture darkened in a way that matters. On June 9, Novanta announced its largest acquisition ever: Riverpoint Medical, for $1.45 billion, funded partly by a dilutive $300 million equity raise, explicitly designed to lift Medical to roughly 60% of total company revenue. That's management spending a quarter of the company's market cap to shrink robotics' share of the business, not grow into the thesis. Two weeks later, NVIDIA formally launched its "Halos for Robotics" program and put Agility Robotics on stage as the flagship customer — Novanta, despite years of ecosystem proximity, was nowhere in the announcement. The numeric score holds at 5/8, same as Fires 33 and 39, but this is now the weakest of three reads, and there's still no named humanoid or AV OEM anywhere in the record after eleven fires of coverage. NOVT is demoted out of the top-5 conviction shortlist to a dormant/kill-adjacent monitoring status — not removed from the pipeline, just no longer where compute gets spent.

That demotion mechanically opens a top-5 slot, which Xinjian Transmission fills back in — not because anything new happened in its favor, but because its Fire 43 terminal-kill trigger was explicitly rechecked this fire (by design, not by default) and confirmed not to have fired. The company is still sitting at "已受理"/accepted on the SZSE ChiNext docket, with no inquiry round yet, no pricing, no withdrawal — the same state it was in last fire, which is expected this early (only about two weeks since acceptance). The pre-committed kill conditions (IPO pricing, an adverse inquiry-round finding, or a stalled filing) remain live and unchanged.

Sourcing itself ran unusually wide this fire and found almost nothing that survived contact with the gates. The interconnect node (this fire's rotation-cursor stop) turned up four new China A-share connector/cable names, every one of them either too large, already re-rated, or resting on a sell-side analyst's inference rather than the company's own words. A UBTECH annual-report re-scan surfaced two more real but already-mega-cap suppliers (Sanhua Intelligent Controls, Lens Technology — both tens of billions of dollars in market cap). The China A-share 互动易 investor-relations sweep found six more names, two of which are worth remembering as a pattern: Lixing Co and Sanfeng Intelligent both had media-reported Tesla/humanoid ties that the companies themselves explicitly denied on their own investor platforms — a reminder that a popular "concept stock" narrative and a real component relationship are not the same thing. The Chinese trade-press sweep found five more kills, all mega-cap (Rockchip, Topgroup, and a Kedali subsidiary among them), plus one interesting near-miss: Choho Industrial (征和工业), which has a real, filed claim of "actual orders" from robotics customers, but the only company it names anywhere is Zhiyuan, shown at a product demo under a capital/strategic-partnership framing, not confirmed as the paying buyer behind that order language. That's logged as a weak PENDING, not a WATCH — a genuinely new failure pattern worth watching for elsewhere.

The most structurally important finding this fire wasn't a company at all — it was a market-timing effect. Unitree's CSRC IPO-registration approval on July 2-3 triggered a broad, multi-day limit-up wave across more than thirty China humanoid-supplier "concept stocks" the very next trading session. Every genuinely new named-buyer disclosure this fire's China-focused scouts found had either already been swept up in that re-rating or was already a mega-cap regardless of the rally. The practical implication: the public China A-share layer for Unitree/UBTECH/AgiBot suppliers is now close to fully mined and increasingly pre-priced on discovery; the more promising remaining vein is private, pre-IPO names — this fire's one genuinely new PENDING lead, Nuttall (纽氏达特), a planetary-reducer maker whose own ChiNext IPO prospectus names Zhiyuan as its largest customer by revenue, is exactly that shape.

One dedup near-miss is worth flagging even though nothing came of it: an EDGAR full-text sweep found Hesai's own SEC filing naming Unitree as a lidar customer — a perfectly real, dated, primary-source disclosure — and the scout initially wrote it up as a "new kill candidate." Hesai is the KB's #1 conviction WATCH name; this was caught and discarded before it reached any permanent file. It's the fifth time in four fires this exact failure mode has appeared in some form, which suggests the fix (orchestrator cross-checks every scout "new" claim against the live WATCH table, not just the kill list) needs to stay a standing, not occasional, discipline.
